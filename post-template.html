Why Avoiding Conflict Costs More Than You Think

Most of us were trained to manage disease, not disagreement. But in healthcare settings, unresolved conflict doesn't stay contained — it spreads.

Back to Blog

In medical training, we learn to act. We diagnose, we intervene, we resolve. But when it comes to interpersonal conflict, most clinicians default to a different instinct: avoidance. Don't escalate. Don't make it awkward. Keep moving.

It's an understandable response. Clinical environments are high-pressure, time-constrained, and deeply hierarchical. Surfacing friction can feel risky — professionally and relationally. So we let things slide. And then we let them slide again.

"Avoiding conflict is not the same as resolving it. In most cases, it is simply deferring the cost."

But here's what the research consistently shows: in healthcare settings, unresolved conflict doesn't stay contained. It compounds. It affects team communication, erodes psychological safety, and — critically — it degrades care quality.

The Hidden Costs

When conflict goes unaddressed, teams don't just feel tense — they function differently. Communication becomes more guarded. People stop speaking up about concerns. Nurses hesitate before flagging an issue to a physician they've had friction with. Residents stay quiet in rounds rather than risk embarrassment.

These aren't abstract dynamics. A 2010 study published in The Joint Commission Journal found that communication failures contributed to over 70% of serious adverse events in hospitals. Not equipment failure. Not clinical error alone. Communication.

Key Takeaway

Conflict avoidance isn't neutral — it's a choice with downstream consequences for your team, your patients, and yourself. The question isn't whether to address conflict, but how.

Why We Avoid

Understanding avoidance requires some self-awareness. According to the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument — the most widely validated tool for assessing conflict styles — avoiding is one of five legitimate responses to conflict. It's not pathological. In some contexts, it's exactly right.

But for many clinicians, avoiding isn't a strategic choice — it's an unconscious default. And defaults are only useful if they're well-matched to the situation.

Common drivers of chronic avoidance in clinical settings include:

  • Fear of damaging a professional relationship
  • Uncertainty about how to raise the issue constructively
  • A belief that the other person won't be receptive
  • Past experiences where speaking up backfired
  • Organizational cultures that implicitly discourage dissent

A Different Frame

Most conflict-avoidant clinicians aren't conflict-averse people — they're people who haven't been given the tools to navigate it well. Medical training is full of technical skills. Interpersonal ones are largely left to chance.

The good news is that conflict competence is learnable. Like any clinical skill, it improves with deliberate practice, feedback, and the right conceptual framework. You don't need to become a different person. You need a better set of tools.

"Conflict handled well doesn't damage relationships. In many cases, it strengthens them."

That starts with understanding your own patterns — how you respond under pressure, where you over-rely on avoidance, and what situations trigger a less-than-ideal response. From there, you can start to expand your range.

That's what this blog is about. Not theory for its own sake — but practical, evidence-based approaches to the human side of healthcare, written for the people doing the work.
