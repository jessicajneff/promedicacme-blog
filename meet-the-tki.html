Meet the TKI: The Conflict Style Assessment Over 10 Million People Have Taken

A short assessment that reveals which conflict handling modes you may be under or overutilizing — and why that matters in healthcare.

Back to Blog
At a Glance
  • Born: 1974
  • Developed by: UCLA researchers Kenneth W. Thomas and Ralph H. Kilmann
  • People who have used it: 10 million+
  • Time to complete: 15 minutes
  • Output: Personalized report identifying your likelihood of using 5 different conflict handling modes

This short assessment asks you to choose between 30 paired statements, revealing which conflict handling modes you may be under or overutilizing. There are five possible conflict handling modes: competing, accommodating, avoiding, collaborating, and compromising.

According to the TKI model, no single conflict style is preferred. Rather, different styles are best suited to different situations. By gaining insights into which styles you might be over or underutilizing, you can develop your skill in identifying and applying the conflict mode most suited to a particular situation.

Why This Matters for Healthcare Providers

In your day-to-day work, you face countless encounters that can be a potential source of conflict — often rooted in hierarchical power dynamics, role ambiguity, and communication breakdowns during high-stakes transitions.

Left unaddressed, these conflicts can compromise patient care through clinical errors, treatment delays, and preventable complications. Furthermore, unresolved conflict fuels burnout, emotional distress, and intent to leave the profession.

Conflict is not inherently negative. When skillfully managed, it serves as a powerful catalyst for innovation, organizational resilience, and the development of high-performing, cohesive healthcare teams.

Current medical training frequently overlooks the complexities of intraprofessional conflict, focusing instead on idealized interprofessional cooperation or generic business models that fail to reflect the unique stressors of a clinical environment. This systemic neglect leaves a vast majority of providers feeling critically undertrained and unprepared to manage the high-stakes disagreements that inevitably arise among colleagues.

What You'll Discover

The TKI helps bridge this gap. When you take the assessment, you'll discover your actual conflict patterns — not what you think they are, but what you actually do under pressure. You'll see which modes you're overusing and which you're avoiding, often in ways that surprise you. This awareness is the foundation for managing conflict more effectively in your professional life.
